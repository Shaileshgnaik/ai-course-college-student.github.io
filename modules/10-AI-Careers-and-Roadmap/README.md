## Topics Covered

### 1. AI Career Landscape Overview

The AI career landscape is broader than most students realise. It is not limited to people with computer science degrees writing neural network code. AI has created demand for professionals who can:
- Build AI systems (engineering, research)
- Apply AI in specific domains (healthcare, finance, law, education, agriculture)
- Manage and direct AI projects (product, strategy)
- Govern and audit AI systems (ethics, policy, compliance)
- Train others to use AI (education, enablement)

The most accessible opportunities for most students are in the **applied AI** space — using AI tools effectively within your existing discipline — rather than in AI research or core engineering, which require advanced technical backgrounds.

### 2. Roles: AI Engineer, Data Scientist, Prompt Engineer, ML Ops, AI Product Manager

**AI Engineer:** Builds AI-powered applications by integrating APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) into software products. Requires software engineering skills and understanding of LLMs. Distinct from ML research.

**Data Scientist:** Analyses large datasets using statistical methods and ML to generate business insights. Strong in Python (pandas, scikit-learn), statistics, and data visualisation.

**Machine Learning Engineer:** Builds, trains, and deploys ML models at scale. More engineering-focused than data science. Requires strong Python, ML frameworks (PyTorch, TensorFlow), and MLOps skills.

**Prompt Engineer:** Designs, tests, and optimises prompts for LLM applications. Often part of a larger AI product team. Requires deep knowledge of LLM behaviour, writing ability, and systematic testing.

**MLOps Engineer:** Manages the infrastructure for deploying and monitoring ML models in production. Combines ML knowledge with DevOps and cloud skills.

**AI Product Manager:** Defines what AI products should do, how they should work, and what problems they should solve. Bridges technical AI capabilities and user/business needs. Requires no deep technical AI knowledge but requires understanding of what AI can and cannot do.

**AI Trainer / Quality Analyst:** Evaluates and improves AI outputs — writing training examples, rating responses, identifying model failures. This is how RLHF (the technique behind ChatGPT's improvement) works in practice.

### 3. AI in Non-Tech Careers (Law, Medicine, Finance, Marketing)

Every profession now has AI-specific roles and applications:

**Law:** AI is used for contract review, legal research, discovery review in litigation, and compliance monitoring. Law firms are hiring "legal technology specialists" who understand both law and AI tools. LegalTech startups are growing rapidly.

**Medicine:** AI is transforming radiology, pathology, drug discovery, and clinical documentation. Roles emerging: clinical AI specialist, health informatics analyst, AI ethics officer in hospital systems.

**Finance:** AI drives algorithmic trading, fraud detection, credit underwriting, and personalised advisory. Roles: quantitative analyst, AI risk specialist, robo-advisory product manager.

**Marketing:** AI for personalisation, content generation, customer segmentation, and attribution. Roles: AI content strategist, marketing analytics specialist, growth hacker using AI tools.

**Education:** AI tutoring platforms, personalised learning, automated feedback. Roles: instructional designer with AI expertise, EdTech product manager, AI curriculum developer.

The pattern across all domains: professionals who combine deep domain expertise with AI fluency will be disproportionately valuable.

### 5. Building Your AI Portfolio

A portfolio demonstrates what you can do, not just what you know. For AI roles, an effective portfolio includes:

**GitHub profile:** Host all your code projects here. Include READMEs that explain what each project does and what AI tools you used. Recruiters look at GitHub profiles for AI/tech roles.

**Documented prompt work:** Screenshots or exports of significant prompt engineering work — study assistants, content generation, AI workflows you've built.

**Projects:** Even simple projects demonstrate initiative and application. The projects in this course (Study Assistant, Career Advisor, Campus Chatbot) are portfolio-worthy if well-documented.

**Writing:** Blog posts, LinkedIn articles, or case studies about what you've learned and built. Demonstrates communication skills and genuine engagement with the field.

**Certifications:** Add completed certifications to your LinkedIn profile immediately.

### 6. Resume and LinkedIn for AI Roles

**Resume AI Skills section:** Add a dedicated section listing specific AI tools and techniques you know — not just "AI" as a broad term. Example entries:
- Prompt Engineering (zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought)
- Microsoft Copilot (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Teams)
- GitHub Copilot (Python, JavaScript)
- ChatGPT / Claude (content generation, data analysis, research)
- Google Colab (Python, ML experimentation)

**LinkedIn:** Add the same skills to your LinkedIn Skills section. LinkedIn Learning certificates populate automatically. Write an "About" section that positions you as someone who actively uses AI in your work. Post occasionally about AI tools you're exploring — this builds visibility in the AI talent market.

### 7. Interview Preparation for AI Jobs

**Technical questions you should be ready for:**
- "What is the difference between machine learning and deep learning?"
- "Explain what a large language model is and how it works"
- "What is prompt engineering and can you give an example?"
- "How would you detect and handle AI hallucinations in a production system?"
- "What are the ethical risks of AI and how would you mitigate them?"

**Behavioural questions:**
- "Tell me about a project where you used AI to solve a problem"
- "Describe a time when AI gave you a wrong answer — how did you handle it?"
- "How do you stay current with AI developments?"

**Portfolio review:** Many AI roles include a task where you demonstrate your ability to use AI tools — be prepared to show your work and explain your prompt design choices.

### 8. Your Personal AI Learning Roadmap

The right roadmap depends on your target career. A general framework:

**Months 1–3 (after this course):**
- Complete one beginner certification (AI-900 or Google AI Essentials)
- Build and document one portfolio project
- Start following 3–5 AI practitioners on LinkedIn
- Subscribe to one AI newsletter (The Rundown AI, TLDR AI, or MIT Technology Review AI)

**Months 4–6:**
- Pursue an intermediate certification aligned with your target role
- Complete a hands-on course on Kaggle or DeepLearning.AI
- Apply AI tools in a real project at college or in an internship

**Months 7–12:**
- Build a second, more substantial portfolio project
- Contribute to an open-source AI project or dataset on Hugging Face
- Start applying for AI-adjacent roles or internships
