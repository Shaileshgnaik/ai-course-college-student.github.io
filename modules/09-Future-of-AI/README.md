# Module 09 — Future of AI

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this module students will:

- Identify emerging AI technologies and trends
- Understand the concept of AGI and its implications
- Analyse how AI is transforming key industries
- Form an informed perspective on the long-term future of AI

---

## Topics Covered

### 1. Where AI Is Headed — Key Trends

Several converging trends are shaping the next phase of AI development:

**Multimodality:** AI systems that can seamlessly process and generate across text, images, audio, video, and code in a single model. GPT-4o and Gemini 1.5 Pro are early examples. This enables far more natural human-AI interaction — you can show the AI something on camera and have a conversation about it.

**Decreasing costs:** The cost of running AI inference (generating outputs) has fallen by more than 100× in two years. This makes AI economically viable for an ever-wider range of applications.

**On-device AI:** Models are becoming small enough to run on smartphones and laptops without internet connectivity. Apple Intelligence, Gemini Nano, and Llama 3 can run locally, enabling private, low-latency AI applications.

**Reasoning models:** A new generation of models (OpenAI's o1, DeepSeek R1) that spend more compute "thinking" before responding, dramatically improving performance on complex reasoning tasks.

### 2. Multimodal AI — Text, Image, Audio, Video Together

Multimodal AI removes the constraint of working with one type of information at a time. Real-world applications being unlocked:

- A doctor photographs a wound and an AI provides differential diagnoses
- A student points their phone at a maths problem and the AI explains how to solve it
- An engineer uploads technical drawings and asks AI to identify compliance issues
- A content creator speaks an idea into their phone and AI generates a complete article with matching images

The convergence of modalities is making AI accessible to people who can't type quickly or who work with inherently visual or audio-based information.

### 3. AI Agents and Autonomous Systems

AI agents are systems that can autonomously take sequences of actions to complete multi-step tasks — browsing the web, writing and executing code, managing files, calling APIs, and interacting with software interfaces.

Unlike a chatbot that answers a single question, an agent can:
- Receive a goal: "Research the top 5 competitors for our product and produce a comparison report"
- Break it into steps: search the web, read pages, extract data, organise findings, write report
- Execute each step using tools: web browser, code interpreter, file system
- Return the completed result

Early agent products include OpenAI's Operator, Anthropic's Claude Computer Use, and Microsoft's Copilot Agents. These are still early-stage and error-prone, but the trajectory is clear.

### 4. Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) — What It Means

AGI refers to an AI system that can perform any intellectual task a human can do, at human level or above, across all domains. It has not been achieved. Estimates for when (or whether) it will be achieved vary enormously — from years to decades to never.

The significance: all existing AI is "narrow" — it does specific tasks well but cannot transfer learning to fundamentally different tasks the way humans do. AGI would change that. An AGI could in principle accelerate its own development, creating a feedback loop with uncertain consequences.

AGI is a legitimate subject of serious research (at Anthropic, OpenAI, DeepMind). It is also subject to significant hype. The appropriate stance for students is: take it seriously as a possibility, apply critical thinking to timelines and claims, and focus on understanding the near-term AI landscape that will definitely affect your career.

### 5. AI and the Future of Work

AI is automating tasks, not jobs — at least in the near term. Research suggests that most occupations include tasks that AI can do, and tasks that AI cannot yet do. The mix varies dramatically by profession.

**Roles most at risk of disruption:** Entry-level knowledge work that involves routine text processing — data entry, basic drafting, simple analysis, customer service.

**Roles most resilient:** Those requiring physical presence, emotional intelligence, complex judgment in novel situations, deep domain expertise, or creative synthesis that goes beyond remixing existing patterns.

**The key insight:** AI is a tool that amplifies human capability. Professionals who know how to use AI effectively will be significantly more productive than those who don't. The goal of this course is to put you in that category.

### 6. Industry Disruption — Healthcare, Education, Finance, Law

**Healthcare:** AI is transforming diagnostics (reading scans and pathology slides), drug discovery (AlphaFold solved protein folding), clinical documentation, and personalised treatment recommendations. Radiologists and pathologists face significant automation of routine work.

**Education:** AI tutors that adapt to individual learning pace and style, automated essay feedback, personalised curriculum generation. The risk: students who outsource all thinking to AI instead of building skills.

**Finance:** Algorithmic trading, fraud detection, personalised financial advice, automated credit underwriting, insurance pricing. Most entry-level financial analysis roles are facing significant automation pressure.

**Law:** Contract review and drafting, legal research, document review in litigation, compliance monitoring. AI has passed the bar exam in multiple jurisdictions.

### 7. AI and Climate / Sustainability

AI has a dual relationship with sustainability:

**AI as a problem:** Training large models has a significant carbon footprint. A single training run for a large model can emit as much CO2 as multiple transatlantic flights. Inference at scale (billions of queries) adds further. Data centres require enormous amounts of water for cooling.

**AI as a solution:** AI is being applied to energy grid optimisation, climate modelling, materials discovery (for batteries, carbon capture, solar cells), precision agriculture, and reducing waste in logistics and supply chains. Google's DeepMind used AI to reduce data centre cooling energy consumption by 40%.

The net effect is actively debated. The answer likely depends on whether AI is applied to accelerate the transition to clean energy, or primarily deployed for consumer entertainment applications.

### 8. Human-AI Collaboration

The dominant paradigm for the foreseeable future is not AI replacing humans but humans and AI working together — each contributing what they do best.

AI is good at: processing large amounts of information quickly, generating many options, finding patterns in data, consistent execution of well-defined tasks, and tireless availability.

Humans are good at: judgment in novel situations, ethical reasoning, emotional intelligence, building trust and relationships, creative synthesis that draws on lived experience, and deciding what actually matters.

The most valuable professionals will be those who can fluidly combine both: using AI to expand their capacity while applying distinctly human judgment to guide, verify, and contextualise AI's contributions.
